# People and Talent Strategy
> Part of the Corporate Intelligence Framework — People and Relationships
>
> This artifact captures how the organization thinks about the people it needs
> to execute its strategy — what capabilities are required, where the gaps are,
> how the organization finds and onboards talent, and how it hires for culture.
>
> This is not an org chart or a roster. For current team structure and individual
> stakeholder context, see Internal-Stakeholders.md. This artifact is forward-looking:
> it connects people strategy to organizational strategy and makes visible the gap
> between who the organization has and who it needs.
>
> **This artifact scales across organizational size.** A solo founder thinking
> through their first hire and an enterprise workforce planning team are both
> working the same problem at different levels of formality. The sections below
> apply at any scale — use the depth that matches your context.
>
> **The affordability constraint is real.** This artifact captures what the
> organization needs and where the gaps are. What it does not prescribe is how
> to resolve the tension between capability needs and what the organization can
> invest. That tension is acknowledged here and resolved in the Business Case
> or financial planning process — not in this document.

## 1. How to Use This Document
> This artifact is maintained against the organization's current strategic direction.
> When Strategy-and-Intent.md changes — new goals, new time horizons, new bets —
> this document should be reviewed to assess whether the capability picture changes.
>
> It is not a hiring plan or a headcount budget. It is the strategic layer that
> informs those plans. A hiring manager building a job description, a founder making
> their first key hire, or an executive team doing annual planning should all be
> able to use this document to orient their decisions.

## 3. Culture and How We Hire for It
> Culture is emergent — it is not defined by a values poster, but by the
> behaviors, decisions, and people the organization accumulates over time.
> This section does not attempt to define culture. It captures what the
> organization has learned about the qualities that predict success here,
> and how that shapes who it looks for.
>
> This is one of the most important sections in this artifact. A single
> mis-hire at a critical role can damage culture in ways that take years
> to recover from. Being explicit about what works — and what doesn't —
> is an act of organizational self-awareness.

### 3.1 What Predicts Success Here
> Based on observation of people who have thrived in this organization,
> what qualities, working styles, or orientations tend to predict success?
> These are not requirements — they are patterns worth understanding and
> testing for in the search process.

- [e.g., Comfort with ambiguity — this organization rarely has complete information before making decisions]
- [e.g., Ownership orientation — people who wait to be told what to do struggle here]
- [e.g., Direct communication — feedback is given and received plainly, without significant softening]

### 3.2 What Has Not Worked
> Equally important: what qualities or working styles have proven to be
> a poor fit, regardless of technical capability? This section is often
> harder to write honestly, but it is more valuable for that difficulty.

- [e.g., People who need significant process and structure to operate — we don't have it yet]
- [e.g., Specialists who are unable or unwilling to work outside their defined lane]

### 3.3 Culture Fit vs. Culture Add
> Hiring purely for culture fit risks building a homogeneous organization
> that is comfortable but not capable of the diversity of thought it needs
> to grow and adapt. This section captures the organization's current
> thinking on the balance between fit and add.

[How does the organization currently think about this balance?
What perspectives, backgrounds, or working styles are underrepresented
and would strengthen the organization if added?]

## 4. Search and Onboarding
> How the organization finds talent and brings it in effectively.
> A great hire who is poorly onboarded is a capability gap that was
> briefly filled. Onboarding is where the investment in search is realized
> or lost.
>
> This section scales to organizational size — a founder making their
> first hire and an enterprise talent team are both accountable for
> the same outcome: the right person, in the right role, able to
> contribute as quickly as possible.
