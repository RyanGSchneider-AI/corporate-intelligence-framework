# Sales Playbook
> Part of the Corporate Intelligence Framework — Sales
>
> The Sales Playbook governs how deals are run — from first meaningful engagement
> through close. It is the execution layer beneath the Go-to-Market strategy:
> where GTM defines the motion and the market, the Playbook defines how to win
> within it.
>
> This artifact is built for the person in the deal — a sales rep preparing for
> discovery, a founder closing their first enterprise customer, or an AI agent
> supporting a sales workflow. It should be specific enough to be useful in
> the moment, not just aspirational in the abstract.
>
> **Relationship to other artifacts:**
> - Executes within the motion defined by **Go-to-Market.md**
> - Draws on **Customer-Personas.md** for buyer understanding
> - Draws on **Competitive-Intelligence.md** for competitive positioning detail
> - References **Reference-Customers-and-Champions.md** for proof points
> - Informs **Product-Chronicle.md** with field intelligence on customer language
>   and objections

## 1. Before the Deal — Qualification
> Not every prospect deserves equal investment. Qualification is the discipline
> of applying sales effort where it is most likely to result in a good outcome —
> for the organization and for the customer.
>
> A bad-fit customer who closes is not a win. They churn, they generate support
> cost, and they don't become references. Qualification protects against that.

## 2. Discovery
> Discovery is the most important part of the sales process.
> A deal won on the back of thorough discovery is easier to close, easier
> to implement, and more likely to result in a successful customer.
> A deal closed without it creates surprises on both sides.
>
> The goal of discovery is not to pitch — it is to understand.
> The best discovery conversations feel like consulting, not selling.

### 2.1 Discovery Objectives
Before leaving a discovery conversation, aim to understand:
- **The problem**: What is the specific pain? How long has it existed? What has been tried?
- **The impact**: What does the problem cost — in time, money, risk, or missed opportunity?
- **The trigger**: What changed that made them look now?
- **The buying process**: Who else is involved? How do decisions get made? What does success look like to the economic buyer?
- **The timeline**: Is there a deadline? What happens if they don't solve this by then?
- **The alternatives**: What else are they considering? What would "do nothing" look like?

### 2.2 Discovery Questions
> Organized by objective. These are starting points — the best discovery
> follows the conversation, not the script.

**Understanding the problem:**
- "Walk me through what today looks like when [relevant process] happens."
- "Where does that process break down or create friction?"
- "How long has this been an issue?"

**Understanding the impact:**
- "What does that cost you — in hours, in errors, in risk?"
- "Has this caused any specific incidents you can point to?"
- "If you solved this completely, what would change?"

**Understanding the buying process:**
- "Who else will be involved in evaluating this decision?"
- "Have you gone through a process like this before? How did it work?"
- "What does a good outcome look like to [economic buyer]?"

**Understanding the timeline:**
- "Is there a date by which you need this solved?"
- "What happens if you don't have a solution in place by then?"
- "What would cause this evaluation to slow down or stop?"

### 2.3 What Good Discovery Produces
A completed discovery should leave the team able to answer:
- Is this a qualified opportunity? *(reference Section 1.1)*
- Who is the champion and who is the economic buyer?
- What is the specific problem and its business impact?
- What does the customer's success look like — in their words?
- What are the risks to this deal closing?

## 3. Pricing Conversation Guidance
> Pricing conversations are where deals are won or lost on value perception.
> The goal is not to defend a number — it is to connect the price to the
> value the customer has already articulated in discovery.
> Reference Go-to-Market.md Section 4 for the pricing model and principles.

### 3.1 When to Introduce Pricing
[At what point in the process does pricing typically come up?
What is the organization's philosophy on when to share pricing —
early to qualify, or late once value is established?
What has been learned about what works?]

### 3.2 Framing Value Before Price
> Price should follow value, not precede it. Before presenting a number,
> make sure the customer has articulated their own pain and the cost of inaction.
> Then anchor the price against what they've already said matters to them.

**The anchoring sequence:**
1. Reflect the problem back in their language: *"You mentioned that [specific pain] costs you [specific impact]..."*
2. Connect the solution to that outcome: *"What we've built specifically addresses that by [specific mechanism]..."*
3. Introduce pricing in that context: *"Given what you're dealing with, here's how we structure the investment..."*

### 3.3 Handling the Price Objection
> "It's too expensive" is almost always a value objection, not a budget objection.
> The response is to return to value, not to discount.

**First response — return to value:**
*"Help me understand — too expensive relative to what? What you're spending now, or what you were expecting?"*

**If budget is genuinely constrained:**
[What options exist? Phased implementation? Smaller initial scope?
What is the organization's policy on structuring deals to fit a constrained budget
without eroding long-term value?]

**If the competitor is cheaper:**
*"What are they including at that price? Let's make sure we're comparing the same things."*
[What are the known areas where lower-cost alternatives cut corners or create hidden costs?]

## 4. Objection Handling
> Objections are buying signals — a prospect who raises objections is engaged.
> The goal is not to overcome objections by force but to understand the concern
> underneath them and address it honestly.
>
> Add entries as new objections emerge in the field. This section should grow
> over time as the organization learns from real conversations.

---

**Objection**: "We're happy with our current solution."
**What it usually means**: Low urgency, not enough pain identified yet — or they haven't considered the cost of their current state.
**Response approach**: Return to discovery. *"That's great to hear — what's working well about it? And are there any areas where it falls short or creates extra work?"* The goal is to surface latent pain without challenging their self-assessment.

---

**Objection**: "We don't have budget right now."
**What it usually means**: Either genuinely no budget, or the value hasn't been established well enough to create urgency to find it.
**Response approach**: *"Understood — when does your budget cycle reset? And if this were the right solution, is this something worth planning for?"* If genuinely no budget horizon exists, qualify out gracefully rather than investing further.

---

**Objection**: "We need to involve IT / Legal / Finance / [other stakeholder]."
**What it usually means**: This is a normal part of the buying process, not a stall — unless it appears repeatedly without progress.
**Response approach**: Welcome it and get ahead of it. *"Absolutely — who should we loop in, and what do they typically need to evaluate something like this? Let's make sure we have the right materials ready."* Proactively preparing for IT security reviews, compliance questions, or finance sign-off prevents late-stage surprises.

---

**Objection**: "Competitor X is cheaper / has more features."
**What it usually means**: They're evaluating alternatives — as they should be.
**Response approach**: *"It's worth comparing carefully. What specifically did they show you?"* Don't attack the competitor — understand what the customer found compelling and address the gap or reframe the comparison. Reference Competitive-Intelligence.md for specific positioning.

---

**Objection**: "We're not ready to make a decision yet."
**What it usually means**: Either genuine timing issue or insufficient urgency established.
**Response approach**: *"That's fine — what would need to be true for this to be the right time? Is there a specific event or milestone you're waiting on?"* This distinguishes a real timeline from a polite brush-off and surfaces what needs to happen to move forward.

## 5. Competitive Positioning in a Sales Context
> This section captures how to position against known competitors
> in a live deal — not a full competitive analysis (see Competitive-Intelligence.md)
> but the practical guidance for what to say and what to avoid when a
> competitor is in the conversation.

### 5.1 Positioning Principles
- **Lead with your strengths, not their weaknesses.** Attacking competitors
  damages trust and shifts the conversation away from your value.
- **Acknowledge what they do well.** A prospect who has evaluated a competitor
  will notice if you pretend weaknesses don't exist. Credibility comes from honesty.
- **Reframe the comparison.** The goal is not to win a feature comparison —
  it is to change what the customer is evaluating.

### 5.2 Competitor Positioning Guide
> For each known competitor, capture: what they do well, where we're stronger,
> how to reframe the comparison, and what not to say.
> Reference Competitive-Intelligence.md for full profiles.

## 6. Partner-Assisted Motion
> When a deal involves a channel partner — reseller, referral, integration
> partner, or distributor — the dynamics differ from a direct sale in ways
> that matter. This section governs how to run deals that involve a partner.
>
> A partner is not just a lead source. They have their own relationship with
> the customer, their own commercial interests, and their own limitations.
> Treating a partner-assisted deal like a direct deal creates friction,
> damages the partner relationship, and often loses the deal.

### 6.1 Partner Discovery
> In a partner-assisted deal, discovery has two layers:
> understanding the customer's problem *and* understanding the partner's
> position, relationship, and commercial expectations.

**Questions to ask the partner before engaging the customer:**
- "How did you come across this opportunity? What's your existing relationship with them?"
- "What have you already told them about us? What are their current expectations?"
- "What does a successful outcome look like for you commercially?"
- "Are there any sensitivities or history with this account I should know about?"
- "Who should lead the customer conversation — you, us, or jointly?"

**What good partner alignment looks like before customer engagement:**
- Agreed on who leads the discovery conversation
- Agreed on commercial structure and partner compensation
- Partner briefed on product capabilities and current limitations
- Deal registered per Go-to-Market.md channel conflict governance

### 6.2 Co-Selling with a Partner
> Co-selling requires clear role definition. Ambiguity in front of the customer
> undermines credibility for both parties.

**Typical role division:**
| Activity | Typical Lead | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Customer relationship and trust | Partner | They own the relationship — respect it |
| Product demonstration and technical depth | Organization | Our expertise to deploy |
| Commercial negotiation | Depends on model | Resellers own the commercial; referral partners do not |
| Implementation and onboarding | Organization | Unless partner is certified |
| Ongoing support | Agree in advance | Customers need to know who to call |

### 6.3 Partner-Specific Objection Patterns
> Partners surface different objections than direct prospects — often
> reflecting their own concerns about margin, support, and competitive fit
> within their portfolio.

---

**Objection**: "We're not sure our customers are ready for this."
**What it usually means**: The partner hasn't been enabled well enough to feel confident positioning it, or they're protecting an existing revenue stream.
**Response approach**: Return to enablement. Offer a joint call or a supported pilot. If they're protecting an existing relationship, understand what it is before proceeding.

---

**Objection**: "The margin isn't enough for us to prioritize this."
**What it usually means**: Genuine economics concern — or the partner has higher-margin alternatives in their portfolio.
**Response approach**: Understand what they're comparing against. Is this a pricing issue, a volume issue, or a perceived sales effort issue? Reference Go-to-Market.md Section 3.1 for partner economics and what flexibility, if any, exists.

### 6.4 Protecting the Partner Relationship
> The partner relationship outlasts any single deal. Behaviors that win
> a deal at the partner's expense are not wins — they are withdrawals from
> a relationship that has long-term value.

**Never do:**
- Go around a partner to close a deal they registered or introduced
- Make commercial commitments to a customer that conflict with the partner's arrangement
- Position the organization as a replacement for the partner's value

**Always do:**
- Keep the partner informed of deal progress, especially if the customer contacts the organization directly
- Honor deal registration and conflict governance rules even when it's inconvenient
- Give the partner credit — internally and with the customer — for deals they enabled

## 7. Reference Customer Usage
> Reference customers are one of the most powerful assets in a sales conversation.
> This section governs when and how to deploy them.
> For the full reference customer list, see Reference-Customers-and-Champions.md.

**When to introduce a reference:**
- When the prospect has articulated a specific concern a reference customer has already navigated
- When segment fit is in question and a peer customer validates it
- When the deal is stalled and a peer conversation would create momentum

**How to make the introduction:**
[What is the process for connecting a prospect with a reference customer?
What does the reference customer need to know before the conversation?
How do you brief the prospect so the conversation is focused?]

**How to protect reference customers:**
[Reference customers are a finite and valuable asset. What is the policy on
how frequently they can be used and for what types of deals?
Overusing a reference customer erodes the relationship.]
