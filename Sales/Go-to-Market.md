# Go-to-Market
> Part of the Corporate Intelligence Framework — Sales
>
> The Go-to-Market artifact captures how the organization reaches, sells to, and
> expands with its customers. It covers the full commercial model — who the
> organization sells to, through what channels and motions, at what price, and
> how it packages what it offers.
>
> This is a strategic document, not a campaign plan or a sales script. It answers
> the question: *how does this organization get its product into the hands of the
> right customers?* The Sales Playbook governs how individual deals are executed
> once the motion is engaged.
>
> **This artifact accommodates any sales motion** — product-led, sales-led,
> partner-led, or any combination. Most organizations use more than one motion
> across different segments or stages of growth. Document what is actually true,
> not what is aspirational.
>
> **Relationship to other artifacts:**
> - Informed by **Customer-Personas.md** — who the organization is selling to
> - Informed by **Strategy-and-Intent.md** — which segments and motions are
>   strategic priorities
> - Informs **Sales-Playbook.md** — the playbook executes within the motion
>   this document defines
> - Informs **Reference-Customers-and-Champions.md** — reference customers
>   validate the GTM narrative

> **Significant Change Log**
> A record of meaningful changes to this document — entries made when the content shifts in a way that would affect how a reader interprets decisions made against it. Routine updates (correcting a date, adding a new entry to a register) do not require a log entry. A log entry is warranted when: the document's overall picture has changed, a core definition or principle has shifted, organizational context that drove earlier decisions has been replaced, or a reader a year from now would need to know that this document meant something different before this date.
>
> When writing an entry, capture not just what changed but what the document reflected *before* — so the log itself is recoverable context, not just a changelog.

## 5. GTM Narrative
> How the organization tells its commercial story.
> This is the bridge between the product's internal identity (Product-Chronicle.md)
> and the external positioning (Brand-and-Narrative.md) as expressed in a
> sales and marketing context.
>
> A strong GTM narrative answers three questions for the buyer:
> why change, why us, and why now.

**Why change:**
[What is the cost of the buyer's current state? What pain or missed opportunity
does inaction perpetuate? Frame this in the buyer's language, not the product's features.]

**Why us:**
[What does this organization offer that alternatives do not?
What is the defensible, honest differentiator — not a feature list,
but the outcome the customer gets that they can't get elsewhere?]

**Why now:**
[What creates urgency? What changes if the buyer waits —
competitive pressure, regulatory deadline, cost of continuing status quo?]
