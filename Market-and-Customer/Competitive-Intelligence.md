# Competitive Intelligence

> A living record of the competitive landscape — from formal competitor profiles to informal signals picked up in the field. This artifact covers not just direct competitors but the full range of alternatives that influence buying decisions, including substitute solutions and the status quo.
>
> **A deal lost to a spreadsheet is still a lost deal.** The alternatives section exists because the most common competitor in many markets is not another vendor — it is inertia, internal resourcefulness, or a fundamentally different approach to the same problem.

> **Significant Change Log**
> A record of meaningful changes to this document — entries made when the content shifts in a way that would affect how a reader interprets decisions made against it. Routine updates (correcting a date, adding a new entry to a register) do not require a log entry. A log entry is warranted when: the document's overall picture has changed, a core definition or principle has shifted, organizational context that drove earlier decisions has been replaced, or a reader a year from now would need to know that this document meant something different before this date.
>
> When writing an entry, capture not just what changed but what the document reflected *before* — so the log itself is recoverable context, not just a changelog.

## 3. Substitute Solutions

> Products, services, or approaches that are not direct competitors — they don't operate in our category — but compete for the same budget and solve enough of the same problem to be a real alternative in the buyer's mind.
>
> These are often underweighted in competitive analysis. A prospect who chooses an adjacent platform with partial overlap, or a consulting firm that offers to build a custom solution, is a lost deal that never shows up in a win/loss report as a competitor.

## 4. Status Quo Alternatives

> The most common "competitor" in many markets: the decision to do nothing, to keep doing what they're doing, or to solve the problem internally without buying anything.
>
> These alternatives rarely appear in win/loss analysis, but they represent a significant portion of pipeline that never closes. Understanding them is essential for both product and sales — if the status quo is genuinely good enough, that is a product signal, not just a sales problem.

### 4.1 Status Quo Profiles

---

#### Status Quo: [Name or Description]

**Type**: [e.g., Manual process / Spreadsheet-based workflow / Internal build / Hired coordinator / Do nothing]
**Frequency**: [e.g., The primary reason deals stall or go dark]

**What it looks like:**
[Describe how prospects are currently solving the problem without a dedicated solution. e.g., Most prospects in our pipeline are managing the workflow in a combination of Excel, email, and shared drives. A part-time coordinator is often responsible for maintaining accuracy. The process works well enough in small organizations and begins to break down between 50–200 users.]

**Why buyers stay with it:**
- [e.g., It is free — switching has a visible cost and the status quo has a hidden cost]
- [e.g., The pain is distributed across the team and no single person owns it enough to champion a change]
- [e.g., A previous implementation attempt failed and the organization is reluctant to try again]
- [e.g., The decision requires organizational alignment that the champion cannot achieve]

**What has to be true for a buyer to leave it:**
- [e.g., A visible failure event — audit finding, missed deadline, compliance incident — that creates urgency]
- [e.g., A new leader who arrives with a mandate to modernize and does not have legacy attachment to the current process]
- [e.g., Volume growth that makes the manual process obviously unsustainable]

**How we accelerate the decision:**
[e.g., We quantify the hidden cost of the status quo — time spent, error rate, staff capacity consumed. We ask: "What would your team do with those hours?" We do not argue against the spreadsheet; we make the cost of the spreadsheet visible.]

**Product implications:**
[What does the status quo tell us about our product? e.g., If prospects stay on spreadsheets because our onboarding is too complex, that is a product problem. If they stay because the pain is genuinely low, that signals we may be targeting the wrong segment.]

---

> Add a status quo profile for each meaningful inaction pattern. Common types:
> - Spreadsheets and manual processes
> - Internal build ("we'll just have engineering do it")
> - Hired headcount to perform the function manually
> - Doing nothing and accepting the problem
> - Waiting — "not this quarter" that becomes "not this year"
